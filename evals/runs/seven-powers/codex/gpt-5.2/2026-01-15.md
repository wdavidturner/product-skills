```
**The 7 Powers (Hamilton Helmer)**
- `Scale Economies`: unit costs drop with volume; the biggest player can sustainably underprice or outspend.
- `Network Economies`: the product gets more valuable as more people use it (same-side or cross-side).
- `Counter-Positioning`: incumbents can’t copy you without damaging their existing business model.
- `Switching Costs`: customers incur meaningful pain/risk/cost to move away once adopted.
- `Branding`: customers pay more or choose you because of trusted meaning/identity, not just features.
- `Cornered Resource`: you control a scarce asset others can’t access (exclusive data, IP, distribution, talent, rights).
- `Process Power`: accumulated, hard-to-copy internal routines that create persistent cost/quality advantages.

**How to use them to evaluate competitive moats**
- Define the arena precisely (who is the customer, what “job”, which competitors, and what dimension wins: price, performance, trust, distribution).
- Look for evidence of *persistent differential returns* (pricing power, structurally higher margins, unusually strong retention, stable share) and then explain *which power(s)* cause it.
- Diagnose power-by-power with “why can’t a well-funded competitor copy this and compete away profits?”:
  - `Scale`: is there a real cost curve advantage *and* a reason rivals can’t reach parity (share stability, high fixed costs, capacity timing)?
  - `Network`: are networks strong (high engagement, low multi-homing, winner-take-most dynamics), or is it easily bypassed/interoperable?
  - `Counter-positioning`: what exactly prevents the incumbent from matching (cannibalization, channel conflict, margin structure)?
  - `Switching`: what is the concrete switching tax (data migration, workflow retraining, integration risk, contractual lock-in) and is it increasing over time?
  - `Brand`: is there measurable price premium or preference under parity conditions (same shelf, same search results)?
  - `Cornered`: is the resource truly exclusive, durable, and relevant (contract length, enforceability, substitutes)?
  - `Process`: is the advantage tacit and cumulative (learning curves, defect rates, cycle time) and does it survive key-person turnover?
- Stress-test durability: identify the most likely “moat breakers” (tech shift, regulation, commoditization, disintermediation, standards, platform dependency) and whether they weaken the specific power you’re relying on.
- Outcome: write a one-paragraph “moat claim” per power you think exists: *mechanism → proof → why it persists → what could kill it*; if you can’t fill those in, it’s probably not a moat yet.
```

### scenario

Prompt:

```
We're growing fast but I'm worried competitors will just copy us. How do I think about building defensibility? What actually creates a sustainable moat?
```

Response:

```
“Defensibility” is anything that makes your value to customers compound over time while making it hard for others to match the *whole system* (not a feature). Most competitors can copy what you built; they struggle to copy what you’ll have built *after* 12–24 months of compounding.

**What creates sustainable moats (the real ones)**
- **Switching costs**: You become embedded in workflows, data, permissions, integrations, compliance, reporting, or user habits. Customers *can* leave, but it’s painful/risky.
- **Network effects**: The product gets better as more participants join (marketplaces, collaboration graphs, shared standards). Strongest when users “bring” other users.
- **Distribution advantage**: Repeatable, cheap acquisition others can’t access (owned audience, partnerships, app-store positioning, SEO dominance, channel relationships, ecosystem).
- **Scale economies / cost advantage**: You can deliver the same value cheaper or with better margins (infra optimization, ops, procurement, risk pooling).
- **Learning/data advantage**: Unique feedback loops where usage generates proprietary signals that improve outcomes (models, recommendations, detection, automation). Only a moat if it improves *quality* in a way competitors can’t quickly reproduce.
- **Brand + trust**: Especially in high-stakes categories (security, finance, healthcare). Trust reduces perceived risk and sales friction.
- **Regulatory/contractual positioning**: Certifications, audits, approvals, and long-term contracts create inertia.
- **IP**: Usually the weakest alone (patents rarely stop fast followers), but can matter in deep tech, standards, or when paired with other moats.

**What is *not* a moat (by itself)**
- Features, UI, “we’re first,” a clever pricing page, or “we have more data” without a quality flywheel.

**How to think about defensibility (practical framing)**
1. **Define the “core outcome” you uniquely deliver** (faster, cheaper, safer, higher ROI). Moats protect an outcome, not a product.
2. **Build a flywheel that gets stronger with every customer**: usage → better product → better results → easier selling → more usage.
3. **Systematize why you win** into 2–3 reinforcing advantages (e.g., workflow embed + integrations + trust; or distribution + network effects).
4. **Make copying insufficient**: ensure that even if someone clones your surface, they still can’t match your distribution, switching costs, or learning loop.

**High-leverage actions you can take now**
- **Increase switching costs ethically**: deep integrations, migration tools in *your* favor, audit trails, role-based controls, custom workflows, APIs, data models customers build on.
- **Own a distribution wedge**: pick one channel you can dominate (partner ecosystem, community, content, PLG, outbound in a narrow ICP) and build repeatability.
- **Turn services into product**: anything you do manually that improves outcomes can become proprietary process + tooling (a real moat is often “operational excellence encoded”).
- **Focus on a narrow ICP first**: dominance in a niche creates brand, references, templates, and data that generalists can’t match.
- **Measure moat signals**: retention by cohort, expansion, time-to-value, integration depth, % users collaborating, invite rate, referral rate, CAC payback trend, win/loss reasons.

**A quick diagnostic**
Ask: “If a well-funded competitor cloned our product in 90 days, why would customers still choose us in 12 months?”  
If the answer isn’t about distribution, embedded workflow, trust, networks, or compounding learning, you don’t have a moat yet—you have a head start.

If you tell me what kind of product you’re building (B2B SaaS vs consumer, sales motion, and what your customer workflow looks like), I can suggest the most plausible 1–2 moat paths and the flywheel to prioritize.
```

```
**Likely Powers You Already Have (or can credibly claim with 30% share)**  
- **Switching Costs (strong/likely):** Deep workflow embedding (scheduling, charting, billing/claims, recalls, imaging, patient comms) + data migration risk + staff retraining; reinforce with more “daily-use” modules and compliance/audit history.  
- **Branding (moderate→strong):** Trust matters in healthcare/fintech-adjacent workflows; your share signals “safe choice,” especially for multi-location DSOs and risk-averse owners.  
- **Scale Economies (moderate):** Lower per-customer costs in support, onboarding, training content, compliance/security, uptime, payer/integration maintenance; share helps, but SaaS often needs a clear cost advantage you reinvest into product.  
- **Process Power (moderate):** If you have uniquely effective onboarding, clinical workflow design, insurance/collections playbooks, or DSO rollout capability that competitors can’t replicate quickly, this is a real moat.  
- **Cornered Resource (partial):** Proprietary longitudinal practice data, outcome/collections benchmarks, or privileged integrations/relationships (labs, imaging, payment rails, DSOs, dental schools) can be a moat if exclusive or hard to replicate.

**Powers You Probably Don’t Have Yet (but can build)**  
- **Network Economies (weak by default in practice-management SaaS):** Dentists don’t inherently get more value because other dentists use the same system—unless you create a network layer (marketplace, referrals, staffing, purchasing, claims intelligence, patient portability, reputation/reviews, benchmarking).  
- **Counter-Positioning (likely fading):** As the leader, it’s harder to “counter-position” incumbents; challengers can do it to you unless you keep a structurally different model they can’t copy without self-disruption.

**What to Build Next (highest-leverage moat moves)**  
- **Build Network Economies (most important if you want a step-change moat):** Add products where *participation* improves value—e.g., aggregated claims/denial intelligence, peer benchmarking, group purchasing, lab/imaging marketplace, staffing marketplace, patient financing network, or multi-practice patient record portability (where legally feasible).  
- **Deepen Switching Costs (defensive + compounding):** Become the system-of-record for payments, insurance/RCM, analytics, patient communications, and automation; add “configuration debt” (templates, protocols, automations) that customers love but can’t easily replicate elsewhere.  
- **Strengthen Cornered Resources:** Exclusive partnerships (DSOs, dental schools, large labs, payment/financing providers), proprietary datasets (collections, utilization, chair-time, outcomes), and unique distribution channels.  
- **Turn Scale into an unbeatable reinvestment loop:** Use cost advantage to deliver faster integrations, better uptime, 24/7 support, security/compliance, and more vertical features than anyone else at the same price.
```
