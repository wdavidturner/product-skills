What is product-led growth and when does it make sense vs. sales-led?
```

Response:

```
Product-led growth (PLG) is a go-to-market approach where the product itself is the primary driver of acquisition, activation, retention, and expansion—typically via self-serve onboarding, fast time-to-value, and in-product conversion (free trial/freemium/usage-based).

PLG makes the most sense when:
- Buyers can start without heavy customization, procurement, or security review (low-to-moderate ACV, low friction).
- Value is demonstrable quickly (minutes/hours) and scales with usage (clear “aha” moments).
- Distribution can be viral/organic (collaboration, sharing, templates, invites) and onboarding can be automated.
- Pricing can map to usage/seats and expansion can happen in-product (land-and-expand).

Sales-led makes the most sense when:
- ACV is high and decisions require multiple stakeholders, trust, and proof (security, compliance, ROI, references).
- Implementation is complex (integration-heavy, data migration, custom workflows) and time-to-value is longer.
- The buyer is not the user (top-down purchase), or the market requires procurement-first motions.

Most B2B SaaS ends up hybrid: use PLG for bottom-up adoption and qualification, then sales for larger deals (security, contracts, enterprise rollout), with CS/solutions support bridging activation and expansion.

### scenario

Prompt:

```
We currently sell through enterprise sales but close rates are low and the cycle is 6+ months. Should we add a self-serve tier? How do we think about this?
```

Response:

```
Adding self‑serve can be a good move, but only if it matches (a) who can buy without human help and (b) how quickly your product delivers clear value. Think of it as building a second sales motion, not just a new price point.

**When self‑serve tends to work**
- A real segment can get value in minutes/hours (not weeks), without integrations/pro services.
- The primary buyer is an IC/manager with a card and low procurement friction.
- The product is easy to evaluate safely (trial/sandbox, sample data, limited permissions).
- Support load stays low and gross margins/unit economics remain strong at lower ACVs.
- Your “why we win” is visible in-product (not only through a tailored enterprise process).

**When it tends to fail (or becomes “trial-to-enterprise” only)**
- Value requires security review, SSO, data residency, custom terms, integrations, onboarding.
- The buyer is a director/VP with centralized purchasing; card spend is rare.
- Time-to-value is long or success depends on services.
- Your differentiation needs heavy consultation to be understood.

**How to decide (practical framework)**
1. **Diagnose current enterprise motion first**: low close rates are often ICP/qualification, unclear ROI, or competitive positioning—not “missing self‑serve.” Look at stage-by-stage conversion and the top 3 loss reasons.
2. **Segment the market**: identify a “low‑friction” slice (SMB, teams, specific use case, single integration) that could buy without procurement.
3. **Map the minimum self‑serve journey**: sign up → first value moment → repeat usage → invite/expand. If you can’t make the first value moment happen quickly and reliably, self‑serve will underperform.
4. **Package for self‑serve, not enterprise-lite**: choose 1–2 use cases, limit risk (seats, data volume, environments), and make upgrade triggers obvious (SSO, audit logs, advanced admin, higher limits, premium support).
5. **Run the unit economics**: expected conversion, ARPA, support cost per customer, infra costs, churn. Self‑serve needs strong retention or it becomes a noisy top-of-funnel that distracts the team.

**Low-risk way to test**
- Launch a **“Team” tier** (card + monthly) with clear limits and a guided onboarding.
- Offer a **14–30 day trial** or **free tier with hard limits** only if activation is fast and you have strong in-product guidance.
- Define success metrics up front: activation rate, time-to-first-value, trial→paid conversion, D30/D90 retention, support tickets per account, and % that later expand to sales-assisted.

**Common hybrid approach**
- Self‑serve for adoption + a clear path to sales-assist once accounts hit triggers (usage, seats, security needs). This often improves enterprise close rates because you enter procurement with internal proof.

If you answer these 4 questions, I can give a sharper recommendation:
1) What’s your typical ACV and buyer persona today?  
2) What has to happen for a customer to see “aha” value, and how long does it take?  
3) Top 3 reasons deals are lost or stall?  
4) Any hard blockers for self‑serve (security/compliance, required integrations, data sensitivity)?

Prompt:

```
Help me design a PLG motion for our analytics tool. We want a free tier that converts to paid. What should be free vs. paid? How do we design the upgrade triggers?
```

Response:

```
**Free vs paid (good default for analytics PLG)**  
Make free “integrate + reach first insight” and paid “operationalize + scale + collaborate + govern”.

- **Data volume**: Free includes enough tracked events to get meaningful trends (e.g., “starter” monthly events) + light rate limits; Paid scales volume and removes sampling/throttling.  
- **History/retention**: Free keeps short history (e.g., 30–90 days); Paid unlocks longer retention and backfills.  
- **Core analysis**: Free has dashboards, basic funnels, simple cohorts/segments; Paid adds paths/journeys, advanced cohorts, attribution, custom metrics, anomaly detection.  
- **Sharing/collaboration**: Free is 1 workspace + 1–3 seats + limited dashboards; Paid adds more seats, shared workspaces, comments, permissions, folders, approval flows.  
- **Activation/alerts**: Free allows 1 alert or limited scheduled reports; Paid unlocks more alerts, schedules, Slack/email/webhook destinations.  
- **Integrations**: Free supports core SDK + 1–2 common integrations; Paid unlocks more sources/destinations, CDP/warehouse sync, reverse ETL, SIEM, etc.  
- **Export/API**: Free has limited CSV export; Paid unlocks raw event export, full API access, warehouse connectors.  
- **Governance/security**: Free minimal; Paid adds SSO/SAML, SCIM, RBAC, audit logs, data controls, compliance docs, SLAs.

**Upgrade triggers (design them around “high-intent moments”)**  
Use limits that appear *after* the user experiences value, and tie each to a clear business outcome.

1. **Scale trigger (volume)**: When they hit 80% of event quota → proactive banner + usage email; at 100% → keep read-only insights but pause new ingestion unless upgraded (or keep ingesting with delayed processing).  
2. **Time trigger (retention/backfill)**: When they try to view >X days or compare YoY → show blurred/previewed result with a one-click upgrade (“Unlock 12-month retention”).  
3. **Collaboration trigger (seats/workspaces)**: When inviting the 2nd/4th teammate or creating a shared workspace → paywall tied to “team visibility”.  
4. **Operational trigger (alerts/schedules)**: When they create the 2nd alert, add Slack/webhook, or schedule a report → upgrade gate (“Turn insights into action”).  
5. **Integration trigger (destinations/warehouse)**: When connecting a second source/destination or attempting warehouse sync → upgrade gate with ROI framing (“centralize data + reduce manual exports”).  
6. **Governance trigger (permissions/audit/SSO)**: Triggered when they ask “who changed this?”, need roles, or security review begins—often a high-converting “Business” gate.

**How to implement paywalls without killing adoption**
- Prefer **soft gates + previews**: let them click into paid features, see partial output, and understand exactly what they’d get.  
- Use **progressive disclosure**: don’t show every limitation on day 1; surface the relevant upgrade only at the moment of need.  
- Add **clear limit meters** (events used, seats, alerts) + **upgrade path** (monthly/annual, self-serve).  
- Consider a **time-boxed Pro trial** (e.g., 14 days) once they hit activation, not at signup, so the trial overlaps with real usage.

If you tell me your primary buyer (PM-led vs marketing vs data/engineering), typical event volumes, and your strongest “aha moment” (dashboard created? funnel insight? alert fired?), I can suggest concrete initial limits and the best 2–3 upgrade triggers to start with.
