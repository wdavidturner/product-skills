## Raw Output

### minimal

Prompt:

```
What is product-led SEO and how is it different from content marketing?
```

Response:

```
Product-led SEO is a strategy where your **product itself generates the pages that rank in search**, rather than relying on editorial content like blog posts.

## Key Difference from Content Marketing

| Aspect | Content Marketing | Product-Led SEO |
|--------|------------------|-----------------|
| **What ranks** | Blog posts, guides, articles | Product pages, user-generated content, data-driven pages |
| **Scales by** | Hiring writers | Growing users/data |
| **Examples** | HubSpot blog, Intercom guides | Zillow listings, Yelp reviews, Canva templates |
| **Effort per page** | High (each piece is handcrafted) | Low (programmatically generated) |

## How Product-Led SEO Works

1. **Programmatic pages** - Your product creates pages at scale from data or user activity (e.g., every Airbnb listing is a page that can rank for "cabin rental in [location]")

2. **User-generated content** - Users create the content that ranks (reviews, templates, profiles, questions)

3. **Data moats** - Proprietary data creates pages competitors can't replicate (Glassdoor salary data, G2 software reviews)

## The Core Insight

Traditional SEO asks: "What content should we create to rank?"

Product-led SEO asks: "How can our product naturally create pages people search for?"

The best examples don't feel like SEO at all—Zapier's integration pages, NerdWallet's calculator tools, and Pinterest's boards all rank because they're genuinely useful product surfaces, not content created primarily for search engines.
```


### scenario

Prompt:

```
We're getting 10k organic visitors/month but almost none convert. Our blog drives traffic but it's not the right audience. Is there a better way to do SEO?
```

Response:

```
Here's what's happening and how to fix it:

## The Diagnosis

You have 10k visitors but no conversions because your blog is likely:

1. **Top-of-funnel content** — generic industry topics ("10 Tips for X", "What is Y?") that attract curious browsers, not buyers
2. **No conversion path** — even if someone lands on your blog, there's no logical bridge to your product
3. **Wrong search intent** — you're ranking for informational queries, not decision-making queries

## The Fix: Product-Led SEO

**Stop asking:** "What keywords can we rank for?"  
**Start asking:** "What product experience does the searcher need?"

### Tactical Changes

**1. Audit your current content ruthlessly**

Look at your top 20 pages by traffic. For each one ask:
- Does someone searching this have a problem my product solves?
- Is there a natural next step from this content to my product?
- Would I pay for this visitor?

If the answer is no, that content is waste — even if it ranks.

**2. Move from top-of-funnel to mid-funnel**

| Instead of | Target |
|------------|--------|
| "What is [category]?" | "[Your tool] vs [competitor] for [use case]" |
| "10 tips for X" | "How to solve [specific problem] with [approach]" |
| Generic guides | Comparison pages, calculator tools, templates |

**3. Create content with a product hook**

Every piece needs to connect naturally:
- Calculator that demonstrates your value prop
- Template that leads to your product workflow
- Comparison where you're a logical option

**4. Measure conversions, not traffic**

Track: Signups, MQLs, or revenue from organic — not visits or rankings. One page that converts at 4% beats 100 pages with 0%.

---

Would you like help auditing your current content to identify what to keep, kill, or redirect? Or do you want to map out what mid-funnel content would actually convert for your specific product?
```


### application

Prompt:

```
Help me build a product-led SEO strategy. We're a job board for remote developers. What programmatic pages should we create, and how do we structure them for search?
```

Response:

```
Good news: **A remote developer job board is an excellent fit for Product-Led SEO.** Job boards are among the canonical examples of successful programmatic SEO (like Indeed, Glassdoor, LinkedIn Jobs). You have a clear SEO journey: developers search for jobs, find your listings, and apply.

## Your Programmatic Page Strategy

### Page Types That Work for Job Boards

Here's what to build, ordered by impact:

---

### 1. **Job Listing Pages** (Your Core Product)
The individual job posts themselves. These are mid-funnel, high-intent.

**URL structure:** `/jobs/[company-slug]/[job-title-slug]`

**Example:** `/jobs/stripe/senior-backend-engineer-remote`

**What makes them rank:**
- Unique job description (not duplicated elsewhere)
- Structured data (JobPosting schema)
- Company details, salary range, tech stack
- Clear apply CTA

---

### 2. **Technology/Skill Pages** (High Volume)
Developers search by their primary skill. This is your biggest programmatic opportunity.

**URL structure:** `/remote-[technology]-jobs`

**Examples:**
- `/remote-python-jobs`
- `/remote-react-jobs`
- `/remote-golang-jobs`
- `/remote-rust-jobs`

**Validation:** Check that people actually search "remote Python jobs" (they do — this is validated demand).

**What to include:**
- Aggregated listings filtered by tech
- Average salary data for that skill
- Number of open positions
- Companies hiring for this skill
- Links to individual jobs

---

### 3. **Role/Level Pages** (High Intent)
Developers also search by seniority and role type.

**URL structure:** `/remote-[role]-jobs`

**Examples:**
- `/remote-senior-engineer-jobs`
- `/remote-frontend-developer-jobs`
- `/remote-devops-engineer-jobs`
- `/remote-engineering-manager-jobs`
- `/remote-staff-engineer-jobs`

---

### 4. **Combination Pages** (Long-tail, High Conversion)
Combine technology + role for highly specific queries.

**URL structure:** `/remote-[technology]-[role]-jobs`

**Examples:**
- `/remote-python-backend-jobs`
- `/remote-react-frontend-jobs`
- `/remote-aws-devops-jobs`

**Important:** Only create these if you have enough listings to populate them. Empty pages hurt you.

---

### 5. **Salary Pages** (Mid-funnel, Research Phase)
Developers research salaries before job hunting.

**URL structure:** `/[role]-salary-remote` or `/remote-[technology]-developer-salary`

**Examples:**
- `/remote-python-developer-salary`
- `/senior-engineer-salary-remote`

**What to include:**
- Salary ranges from your actual job data
- Breakdown by experience level
- Comparison to on-site roles
- Companies at each pay band

**Why this works:** Developers researching salaries are in consideration phase — convert them with "see jobs paying $X+" CTAs.

---

### 6. **Company Pages** (Branded Search)
When someone searches "Stripe remote jobs," they should find you.

**URL structure:** `/companies/[company-slug]` or `/[company-slug]-remote-jobs`

**Examples:**
- `/companies/stripe`
- `/companies/gitlab`
- `/stripe-remote-jobs`

**What to include:**
- Company overview (remote culture, benefits, tech stack)
- All open remote positions at that company
- Salary ranges if available
- Reviews/ratings if you collect them

---

## What NOT to Build

Based on the Product-Led SEO framework, avoid:

| Page Type | Why to Skip |
|-----------|-------------|
| "What is remote work?" content | Top-of-funnel, AI Overviews will eat this |
| Generic career advice blogs | Doesn't convert, not your expertise |
| City-based pages ("Remote jobs in Austin") | Remote jobs are location-agnostic by definition |
| Year variations ("Python jobs 2024") | Adds no value, looks spammy |
| Thin pages with <5 listings | Better to consolidate or hide until you have inventory |

---

## Page Structure Best Practices

For each programmatic page type:

```
[H1] Remote {Technology/Role} Jobs

[Count] open positions | Average salary: $X-Y | Updated: {date}

[Filter/Sort Controls]
  - By salary range
  - By experience level  
  - By company size
  - By time zone friendliness

[Job Listings]
  Each with: Company, Title, Salary, Tech Stack, Posted Date

[Salary Data Section]
  Aggregated from your listings

[Related Pages]
  Links to: other technologies, roles, related combinations

[SEO Content Block - keep brief]
  1-2 paragraphs about this role/tech in remote context
  NOT keyword-stuffed filler
```

---

## Technical SEO Essentials

| Element | Implementation |
|---------|----------------|
| **JobPosting Schema** | On every individual job listing |
| **BreadcrumbList Schema** | Show hierarchy (Home > Python Jobs > Senior Python Jobs) |
| **Canonical URLs** | Prevent duplicate content across filters |
| **Pagination** | Use proper next/prev or infinite scroll with crawlable links |
| **Internal linking** | Every job links to its tech page, role page, company page |
| **XML sitemap** | Submit all programmatic pages, update frequently |
| **Index management** | Noindex pages with <3 listings; index when populated |

---

## Metrics That Matter

Don't measure rankings or traffic. Measure:

- **Job applications started** from organic search
- **Applications completed** by landing page
- **Application-to-hire rate** by page type
- **Revenue** (if you charge employers)

---

## Build Sequence

1. **Start with job listing pages** — your core inventory
2. **Add technology pages** — highest volume searches
3. **Add role pages** — capture role-based searches
4. **Build company pages** — capture branded searches
5. **Add combination pages** — only where you have inventory
6. **Add salary pages** — capture research-phase visitors

Each page should be a product, not just content. Invest in the filtering, sorting, and UX that makes these pages genuinely useful to developers.
```
